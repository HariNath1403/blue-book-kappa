    Creatinine Clearance

    Calculate the Estimated GFR

    Or
    mg/dL μmol/L

    References

    Creatinine Clearance (ml/min)
    — Cockcroft-Gault Equation

    • Male:
    • CrCl = (140 - age) * weight (kg) * 1.23 SCr (μmol/L)
    • Female:
    • CrCl = (140 - age) * weight (kg) * 1.04 SCr (μmol/L)

    Estimated GFR (ml/min/1.73 m2)
    — MDRD Equation

    • eGFR =
      175 *       ( SCr 88.4 ) -1.154 * Age -0.203 * Sex * Race
    • Sex
    • Male: 1 & Female: 1.212
    • Race
    • African American: 1.159, Else 1
    Stage CrCl Summary
    Stage 1 ≥ 90 ml/min Normal or Proteinuria (but normal GFR)
    Stage 2 60 - 89 ml/min Kidney damage without symptoms
    Stage 3A 45 - 59 ml/min Kidney function starts declining but no severe symptoms yet
    Stage 3B 30 - 44 ml/min Significant loss of kidney function, with emerging symptoms (e.g., fluid retention, hypertension)
    Stage 4 15 - 29 ml/min Significantly impaired — potential for complications (e.g. hyperkalemia, anemia)
    Stage 5 < 15 ml/min ESRD — requiring dialysis or kidney transplant

    Corrected Sodium

    For Hyperglycaemia

    References

    • Corrected sodium (mmol/L) =
    • Sodium + [0.288 * (Glucose - 5.5)]

    • Source: Sarawak Handbook of Medical Emergencies 3rd Edition

    Correlation Table

    Glucose Level (mmol/L) Effect on Sodium Corrected Sodium Status
    5.5 (Normal) No correction needed Sodium reflects true status.
    6 - 10 Slight decrease Corrected sodium remains close to measured sodium, usually no clinical significance.
    10 - 20 Moderate decrease Corrected sodium is higher than measured sodium. Check for mild dilutional hyponatremia.
    > 20 Significant decrease Marked dilutional effect on sodium. Corrected sodium must be calculated to guide therapy.

    Summary Table

    Sodium Level (mmol/L) Classification Patient's Status
    < 120 Severe Hyponatremia Life-threatening. Risk of seizures, coma, and brain herniation. Requires urgent treatment.
    120 - 129 Moderate Hyponatremia Significant sodium deficit. Symptoms like confusion, nausea, and muscle cramps may occur.
    130 - 135 Mild Hyponatremia Borderline low. Often asymptomatic but may worsen in conditions like heart failure or cirrhosis.
    135 - 145 Normal Range Normal sodium homeostasis.
    146 - 150 Mild Hypernatremia May cause mild symptoms like thirst or lethargy. Requires monitoring and correction of underlying causes.
    151 - 160 Moderate Hypernatremia Risk of neurologic symptoms, including confusion and muscle twitching. Needs prompt intervention.
    > 160 Severe Hypernatremia Life-threatening. Risk of brain shrinkage, seizures, and coma. Requires urgent treatment.

    Ganzoni Equation

    For Iron Deficiency Anaemia (IDA)

    References

    Ganzoni Equation — Fe Deficit

    • Adults & Children (≥ 35 kg):
    • W * (Hb Target - Hb Actual) * 2.4 + 500
    • Children (< 35 kg):
    • W * (Hb Target - Hb Actual) * 2.4 + (15 * W)

    MAP Classification

    MAP Status Interventions
    < 60 (Hypotension / Low MAP) Inadequate organ perfusion, risk of organ failure, and ischemia. Increase IV fluids, use vasopressors, and treat the underlying cause.
    60 - 70 (Borderline Low MAP) Suboptimal perfusion; organ function may be at risk in critically ill patients. Monitor closely, consider fluid resuscitation or vasopressor therapy.
    70 - 100 (Normal MAP) Adequate organ perfusion. Normal function for most patients. No intervention needed if the patient is stable.
    > 100 (Elevated MAP) Increased cardiac workload; risk of hypertension-related complications. Use antihypertensives, diuretics, or treat the underlying cause.
    > 120 (Hypertension / High MAP) Risk of organ damage, including stroke, heart failure, or kidney damage. Immediate intervention with antihypertensives or critical management.

    Potassium Deficit

    In hypokalemic patients

    References

    • Potassium Deficit (mmol/L) =
    • (KNormal - KMeasured) * BW * 0.4

    • Source: Medicine Hack

    Summary Table

    Classification Patient's Status
    Severe Hypokalemia (<2.5 mmol/L) Life-threatening. Risk of arrhythmias, muscle paralysis, and respiratory compromise. Requires urgent correction.
    Moderate Hypokalemia (2.5 - 3.0 mmol/l) Significant depletion. Symptoms like muscle weakness, fatigue, or ECG changes may appear. Needs prompt treatment.
    Mild Hypokalemia (3.1 - 3.5 mmol/l) Borderline low. Usually asymptomatic but may worsen underlying conditions (e.g., arrhythmias in heart disease).
    Normal Range (3.6 - 5.0 mmol/l) Optimal potassium level for most patients. Indicates normal potassium homeostasis.
    Mild Hyperkalemia (5.1 - 6.0 mmol/L) Often asymptomatic. Requires monitoring, especially in kidney impairment or drug-related causes.
    Moderate Hyperkalemia (6.1 - 7.0 mmol/L) May cause ECG changes (e.g., peaked T waves), muscle weakness, and risk of arrhythmias. Requires urgent treatment.
    Severe Hyperkalemia (>7.0 mmol/L) Life-threatening. Immediate treatment is critical to prevent cardiac arrest.
